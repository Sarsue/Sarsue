About Me

Personal Journey

The house TV disappeared after USA 1994 world cup, just like that—replaced by books, discipline, and faith. At five years old, my protests didnt count. My days filled with football matches, choir rehearsals, and math drills. But everything changed in 1997 when a bulky personal computer arrived at our home. My father, an accountant, used it for work, but I was mesmerized by the blinking cursor on the screen. At first, I played games, but soon I was navigating Excel spreadsheets and Access databases, sitting beside my dad as he explained formulas and data structures. I was hooked before I even knew what programming was.

School came naturally—science, math, literature—but when my dad asked about my future, I rattled off grand ambitions of becoming a millionaire. He chuckled, shaking his head, “That’s not a profession.” Engineering seemed like the sensible choice, promising high salaries in oil and gas. So, I followed the tide, unaware that technology had already chosen me.

Preparing for the SATs, I had one foot in the U.S. education system when my parents stumbled upon a Canadian high school program that accepted the WAEC. Within a year, I was in Canada, skipping through courses and finishing high school ahead of schedule. One class, Pascal programming, changed everything. The teacher wasn’t just explaining syntax—he was teaching us how to think. Each challenge felt like a puzzle waiting to be solved, and I couldn’t get enough.

When university admissions redirected me from Petroleum Engineering to Computer Systems Engineering, I took it as a sign. My first semester, ECOR 1606, was an electrifying dive into problem-solving. Then came the class with a 3D printer project that felt like something out of science fiction, and the thrill of building real-world solutions. I learned to balance nights of debugging with campus life, piecing together an identity that was equal parts engineer and explorer.

For my final year project, I built an exam scheduling system. It had potential—real potential. But after graduation, I shelved it, drowning in job applications instead. Years later, as I watched scheduling software carve out entire industries, I often wondered: What if I had believed in my idea just a little more?

Professional Experience

My career started at a startup specializing in situational command and control (SCC). In those eight years (2011-2019), I wasn’t just coding—I was solving problems that had life-or-death consequences. One of my proudest contributions was the Echo, a satellite device designed for secure, beyond-line-of-sight (BLOS) communication used by special forces. Seeing it integrate seamlessly with the Track24 SCC TITAN system was more than an achievement—it was proof that technology could be a silent guardian in the most critical situations. That work led to a U.S. patent, a milestone that reinforced my passion for building solutions that truly matter.

In 2019, AI and machine learning pulled me into Contextere , where I led the development of an AI-driven platform for blue-collar workers. I wasn’t just coding—I was bridging the gap between business leaders, engineers, and end-users, ensuring that technology wasn’t just innovative, but actually useful. Every project was a lesson in translating ideas into scalable, impactful software.

Present Focus

Today, I work as a freelance software engineer, specializing in scalable solutions that solve real business challenges. My focus has shifted deeper into AI, where I’m building SynTextAI—a platform designed to extract meaningful insights from documents using AI. It’s more than just another tool; it’s about unlocking the hidden potential in information, making decisions faster and smarter.
